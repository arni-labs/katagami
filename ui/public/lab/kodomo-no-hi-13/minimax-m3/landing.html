Nobori Festival is a three-day celebration of childhood courage in the riverside gardens of Sumida. Koinobori on every pole, dragon dances at dusk, and a community that shows up for its kids.

The festival

A weekend built
around one question.

What does it mean to wish a child well? Every program at Nobori answers it differently — through art, food, sport, and the simple act of standing together under a sky full of carp.

01 / Carp pole raising

Sixty poles.
One river.

On May 3, families raise their own koinobori along the Sumida riverbank. Each pole carries three carp — black for the father, red for the mother, and a smaller fish for each child in the household.

02 / Dragon dance

From carp to
dragon, at dusk.

The traditional transformation — the carp climbing the waterfall to become a dragon — is performed live by the Asakusa troupe on May 4. Children are invited to join the procession.

03 / Children's day market

Made by kids,
for everyone.

A weekend market of stalls run by children under 14 — origami, calligraphy, hand-bound notebooks, fresh pastries. Every purchase supports a young maker's first savings account.

The tradition

Why carp
climb the
waterfall.

The story of Kodomo no Hi is the story of a fish that refused to swim downstream. In the old Chinese tale that traveled to Japan in the eighth century, a school of carp spent their lives swimming against the current of the Yellow River. One by one, most turned back. A few kept going — all the way to the waterfall at the source, where the current was so strong it should have killed them. Instead, the carp that climbed the falls transformed into dragons.

"The wish is not that the child becomes powerful. The wish is that the child keeps
swimming upstream, even when the water is strong."

In Japan, the carp became the symbol of that wish. Families raise koinobori — long, cylindrical windsocks shaped like swimming carp — outside their homes in early May, each one a quiet prayer that the children inside will have the courage to climb their own falls. The black carp at the top is the father; the red carp, the mother; the smaller carp below, the children themselves.

Nobori Festival is our version of that tradition, scaled for a city. We don't believe in nostalgia — we believe in the carp. Three days, twelve programs, sixty poles, and one river. The rest is up to whoever shows up.
