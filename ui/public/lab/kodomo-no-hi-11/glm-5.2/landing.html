The legend behind the streamers

A carp swims upstream. A dragon rises.

Long before there were streamers, there was a story. A single carp chose the hardest river — the one that ends in a waterfall called Ryūmon, the Dragon Gate. It climbed, against the current, against the odds. And at the top, it became a dragon.

That is why families fly koinobori on Children's Day. Every streamer is a wish: that your child will have the courage to climb their own waterfall.

What Ryūmon does for your family

Three ways to celebrate the climb.

Ryūmon turns the old ritual into something you can hold in your hand — a living record of each child's courage, growth, and the people cheering them on.

Digital koinobori for each child

Design a streamer for every child in your family — choose the carp, the colours, the pole height. Each one flies on your family's page and animates in the spring wind.

Design a streamer

Growth milestones, tracked all year

First steps, first kanji, the day they rode without training wheels. Log milestones with a photo and a date; Ryūmon plots them on a growth chart that rises like the carp's journey.

See the growth chart

Family sharing, across generations

Grandparents in another city can watch the streamers fly and leave courage notes on each milestone. Ryūmon keeps the whole family on the same river, no matter how far apart.

How a family gets on the river.

01

Create your family

Sign up, add each child by name and birthdate. Ryūmon assigns every child their own koinobori pole on your family page.

Takes 2 minutes. No payment, no trial clock. Free for every child, forever.
02

Track the milestones

Through the year, log firsts and favourites with a photo. Each entry lands on the growth chart and sends a courage note to the family circle.

Avg: 1 entry per week. Grandparents get a push notification each time.
03

Celebrate on May fifth

On Kodomo no Hi, every child's streamer flies at full height. The family page plays the year back — a highlight reel of the climb.

One day, one river. The whole family watches the streamers rise together.
One family's story

Mei's first kanji, and the streamer that watched it happen.

The Tanaka family joined Ryūmon when Mei was four. Her father designed a small indigo carp streamer — the same colour as the one his own grandfather flew for him. Over the year, they logged 47 milestones: the first time she wrote her name in hiragana, the day she counted to one hundred, the afternoon she finally rode her bike down the hill without help.
