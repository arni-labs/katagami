Koinobori

The streamers rise

Across Japan, rivers and rooftops come alive with koinobori — carp-shaped wind socks that swim and pull against the May breeze. Each streamer on the pole carries meaning: the black carp for the father, the red for the mother, and a new colour for every child in the family.

The carp was chosen because it swims upstream against the strongest current. On this day, every child is a carp — rising, determined, unstoppable.

Traditions of the day

Customs of Kodomo no Hi

The day weaves together ancient ritual and family joy. Every custom carries a wish: for strength, for health, for a future that rises.

Kabuto display

Families set out samurai helmets and warrior dolls — not for war, but as a wish for courage and resilience in every child who sees them.

Shobu-yu bath

An iris-leaf bath on the eve of the festival. The sword-shaped leaves were believed to ward off illness and invite good health through the coming year.

Koinobori raising

The tallest pole goes up first. Families raise their streamers together — each carp a member of the household, swimming as one against the wind.

Festival table

Kashiwa-mochi and chimaki

No Kodomo no Hi is complete without its foods. Kashiwa-mochi — sweet rice cakes wrapped in oak leaves — symbolise family continuity, because oak trees never shed old leaves before new ones grow.

Chimaki, bamboo-leaf wrapped rice dumplings, carry a different wish: protection from harm. Together they make the festival's table — simple, seasonal, full of meaning.

The carp swims upstream. The child grows strong. The family rises together.
A wish spoken every fifth of May

A day for every child

The feeling of the festival

Kodomo no Hi is not a performance. It is a day when families pause, look up at their streamers, and remember what matters: that every child deserves the chance to rise.

Morning: raise the streamers

The day begins with the pole. Families gather to hoist their koinobori — the tallest carp first, then each smaller one in order. By mid-morning, entire neighbourhoods are alive with colour.

Midday: share the table

Kashiwa-mochi and chimaki, green tea, and time together. Some families visit shrines; others picnic beneath the streamers. The food is simple. The company is the point.

Evening: the iris bath

As the day winds down, the shobu-yu bath closes the circle. Sword-shaped iris leaves float in warm water — an ancient wish for health, carried into the modern evening.
