The rising stream

Koinobori climb the May sky

On bamboo poles along the riverbank, carp-shaped streamers ascend in a fixed family order — black for the father, red for the mother, then one blue child-fish for each son in the household. They billow in the early-summer wind, a living ladder of aspiration.

At Nobori Festival, over two hundred streamers rise above Sumida River Park. Watch them from the meadow at noon, or walk the pole-lined path at golden hour when the light turns electric.

Traditions

Customs of the fifth day

Kodomo no Hi honours children's health and happiness. Families display armour dolls called gogatsu ningyo, bathe in shobu iris leaves for strength, and fly koinobori so children may grow as resilient as carp swimming upstream.

  • Display

    Kabuto helmets

    Miniature samurai helmets sit in household alcoves — symbols of courage passed between generations.

  • Ritual

    Shobu baths

    Iris leaves steep in morning bathwater. Their sharp scent is said to ward off misfortune for the year ahead.

  • Gathering

    Koinobori raising

    At dawn, volunteers hoist the first streamer while taiko drums mark the official opening of the festival grounds.

Food of the day

The Nobori kitchen tent serves seasonal dishes tied to May traditions — each one wrapped, steamed, or leaf-pressed with purpose.

Chimaki

Bamboo-leaf parcels of glutinous rice, steamed until fragrant. Unwrap at the meadow picnic tables.

Kashiwa mochi

Soft rice cakes wrapped in oak leaves — the leaf is not eaten, but its imprint flavours the mochi within.

Koinobori yaki

Festival wagashi shaped like miniature carp, glazed in sky-blue and coral icing.

Shobu somen

Fine white noodles chilled and served beside fresh iris petals — a cooling midday dish.

Early summer arrives on a diagonal — the wind lifts the carp, the children run beneath, and for one bright day the whole city looks upward.

Nobori Festival is free and open to all. Gates open at 08:00. The final streamer descends at sunset.
