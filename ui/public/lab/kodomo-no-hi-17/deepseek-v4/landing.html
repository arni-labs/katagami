The Day

A thousand carp rise on the fifth of May

Kodomo no Hi is the close of Golden Week — a day when families pray for the health, strength, and happiness of their children. Across Japan, rivers of fabric carp stream from poles and bridges, each one a wish carried on the wind.

  • Koinobori — The Carp Streamers

    Cloth carp flown from poles, each representing a family member. The largest, black magoi, is the father; the red higoi, the mother; smaller streamers follow for each child. They swim upstream against the wind — a symbol of strength and perseverance.

  • Gogatsu Ningyo — The Warrior Dolls

    Families display miniature samurai armour and kabuto helmets in their homes. These are not toys but talismans — the armour is believed to guard children against illness and misfortune through the year ahead.

  • Shōbu — The Iris Festival

    Iris leaves are steeped in baths — shōbu-yu — believed to ward off evil. The word shōbu also means "martial spirit," tying the flower to the samurai virtues the day honours.

Customs & Colour

The traditions that fill the morning

From the streamers raised at dawn to the sweets shared in the afternoon, every ritual of Kodomo no Hi carries generations of meaning.

Kabuto & Yoroi

Samurai helmets and armour displayed in the tokonoma alcove. Crafted in intricate detail — lacquer, silk cord, gilded crests — each piece a wish for a strong and honourable life.

The Festival Table

Kashiwa mochi wrapped in oak leaves that never fall until new buds push them out — a symbol of unbroken lineage. Chimaki, the bamboo-leaf dumpling from the legend of Qu Yuan, ties the day to an older Chinese tradition of protection.

A Gathering of Generations

Grandparents, parents, and children come together in parks and gardens. The streamers overhead, the breeze carrying laughter — the day is as much about family presence as about the children it honours.
