Kodomo no Hi

The wind takes the streamers upward before the sun clears the trees.

The Rising

Families raise the streamers while the air is still cool. The black carp for the father flies at the top of the pole. Blue follows for the eldest son, green and red for the younger children. The fabric fills and the spines flex. For a moment the whole line stands straight against the sky.

The order on the pole

Black (father) · Red or pink (mother) · Blue (eldest son) · Green · Orange or red (younger sons and daughters). The order has softened over the years; the lift remains the same.

When they go up

Before dawn on the fifth. By mid-morning the river path is lined. They stay until the evening of the sixth or until the wind turns.

The helmet stands on the grass while the first streamers are tested on the poles.

The Day

Kodomo no Hi began as Tango no Sekku, the boys' festival. It now belongs to every child. The carps fly for all of them. The helmet and armor remain on the stand, but the streamers have become the day's true signature.

Before light

The poles are carried from storage. Sake and tea are poured for the first raising. Children hold the ropes while adults steady the base.

Mid-morning

The river field fills. Grandparents sit on folding stools. The youngest run between the poles, counting the colours aloud.

After three

The wind often drops. The streamers sag and then fill again when the breeze returns from the bay. The day ends when the light turns gold on the fabric.

At the Table

The same foods return every year. They are simple, tied to the season, and meant to be eaten with the hands while standing in the grass.

  • Kashiwa-mochi Oak-leaf mochi filled with sweet red bean. The leaf is not eaten; its scent stays on the fingers.
  • Chimaki Steamed sweet rice wrapped in bamboo leaves and tied with red-and-white thread. Unwrap slowly.
  • Koi sweets Small fish-shaped cakes in red, white, and green. One for each streamer on the pole.

In the Wind

The real event is the space between the poles. People walk slowly, heads tilted back. Conversations happen in the gaps where the fabric snaps.

The first raising

One family at a time. The pole is walked upright. A child is lifted to tie the final knot. Everyone claps once when the line is straight.

The river path

Stalls sell chilled barley tea and grilled squid. The path stays open until the last light. Shoes fill with dry grass.

Evening

The streamers are left up. A few lanterns are lit along the water. The carps turn slowly in the last breeze and then hang still.

The full day is written down.

Times, places, and the map of every streamer pole. Everything you need to walk the grounds without missing the wind.
