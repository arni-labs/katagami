The fifth of May

A day that wishes children up and out into the world.

Kodomo no Hi — once Tango no Sekku, the Boys' Festival — is now the day Japan celebrates every child. Families raise koinobori, carp-shaped streamers, on tall poles by the house and along the river. The wind fills them and they swim, nose to the sky, all afternoon.

The carp is chosen on purpose. It is the fish that swims upstream, that climbs the waterfall, that — in the old story — becomes a dragon at the top. To fly one for your child is to wish them that same strength: to push against the current and rise.

The koinobori rise

One pole, one family,
each carp a wish.

Read the pole from the top down. The crown is the fukinagashi — the five-colour windsock that catches the wind first and gives this language its name. Below it comes the black magoi, the father; then the red higoi, the mother; then a smaller blue or green carp for each child.

When the breeze comes off the water they all turn together and swim, and the whole riverbank turns into a moving shoal. It is the most ordinary kind of magic — paper and cloth and wind, doing exactly what a wish is supposed to do.

The whole point

A wish you can see from a mile off, flying in the open sky.

Inside the house

Three customs, kept every year.

Not everything happens on the pole. Indoors, the day has its own quiet objects and rituals.

兜

Kabuto helmet

A samurai helmet — real metal or folded paper — set on a stand. Not for war, but for what armour stands for: keep this child safe, and give them something to stand behind.

菖蒲

Shōbu iris

Sharp iris leaves go into the bath — shōbu-yu. The plant shares its sound with "martial spirit," and its blade-shape was always read as protective. The water smells green and clean.

五月

Warrior dolls

Small musha-ningyō are arranged on a shelf for the season — a heroic figure, a drum, a bow. They come out in late April and go away soon after the fifth, like the carp.

Why armour, why iris

Protection you can set on a shelf.

The objects of Kodomo no Hi are gentle disguises for one wish: let this child grow up strong and unharmed. The helmet is protection. The iris is protection. The carp is courage. None of it is loud — it is a family quietly hoping, out loud, once a year.

You'll find a helmet on the Kabuto Stage at the grounds, and a shōbu-leaf table where the children can take a leaf home for the bath that night.

What we eat

Two sweets, wrapped in a leaf.

Kashiwa-mochi

A rice cake filled with sweet bean paste and wrapped in an oak leaf. The oak holds its old leaves until the new ones come — a quiet symbol of a family line that doesn't break.

Chimaki

Sweet rice steamed in a bamboo or reed leaf, twisted into a long cone. Older than the festival itself, and brought along the river from the south.

At the grounds

Both are made fresh on Food Lane through the afternoon, alongside cold barley tea and the first cold things of summer.

The feeling

屋根より高い — higher than the rooftops. You stand below, your child on your shoulders, and for one bright afternoon the wish is simply, plainly, flying.
