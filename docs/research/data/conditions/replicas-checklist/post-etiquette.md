The discovery of the keeper's logbook has set the whole town to talking, and talking, as usual, has quite outrun good sense. Let us be plain about what he actually did. For forty years he recorded each night twice — once in plain report, once in language fit for a poem. There is nothing improper in this, whatever the neighbours may whisper over their cups. A record kept for one's superiors should be exact, sober, and free of ornament, so that any reader may know at a glance what passed. A record kept for oneself answers to a wholly different rule. It may be as warm, as fanciful, as full of private feeling as its writer pleases, for it asks nothing of anyone and imposes upon no one.

The keeper never once confused the two. He never dressed a report to his masters in the colours of his own page, never let his official hand grow careless because his other hand ran free. That is not madness. That is a kind of good manners practised alone, on a rock, with no company at all to admire it. The town would do well to speak less of his wits and more of his tact, which outlasted him and puts their idle gossip to shame.
