You will hear, before the week is out, of the old keeper and his curious double logbook, and I would have you consider carefully what the odd thing teaches. The facts are these. A man kept a coastal light for forty years, and every night of those years he wrote the same night down twice — once as a sober clerk, and once as an unashamed poet. His plain entry served the Trinity House faithfully; his ornamented entry served nobody on earth but himself. The town, predictably enough, has seized upon quite the wrong question. They ask whether the man was mad or merely vain, which is the sort of lazy question idle people raise when they wish to feel superior to a better man than themselves. Ask what he understood. He understood that a gentleman owes the watching world an exact account of his conduct, and owes himself something finer — a private reckoning in which the day is not merely survived but genuinely redeemed. Keep both your ledgers so.

The keeper did exactly that for forty faithful years and troubled no living soul. Do as he did, and you will pass for honest among men and stay interesting to yourself, which is much the rarer and more difficult accomplishment of the two.
