The Beacon service writes two parallel log streams from a single process. One is terse and machine-parsable. The other narrates every event in full sentences. Both describe identical operations. This duplication predates the entire current on-call rotation, and no surviving document explains its original rationale.

Origin traces to a maintainer who distrusted compact formats and quietly kept a readable narrative beside the structured feed, refreshing each by hand for years. Nobody pruned either output after his departure. Downstream dependence has visibly diverged. Dashboards ingest only the tabular rows. Responders favor the prose whenever an incident turns genuinely strange. Two teams parse the same events differently, and their panels occasionally disagree during postmortems. That discrepancy already misled an investigation. Storage roughly doubled. Query latency suffers wherever batch jobs scan both copies at once. Our recommendation avoids abrupt deletion. Mark the verbose channel deprecated. Freeze its schema. Migrate the small handful of genuine readers onto annotations, retire that leg next quarter after telemetry confirms no live consumers, and keep exactly one archived snapshot for auditors and any curious historian who wanders through later. Ownership transfers to the platform team on completion.
