Examined the keeper's logbook today, at the request of the historical society, and set down my observations as I would for any specimen. The doubling is regular, complete, and evidently deliberate. It runs unbroken from the first winter to the last.

Below each factual line the writer has entered a second version of the same night. The upper notation gives wind, hour, the condition of the lamp, and any vessel within sight of the lantern. That lower line repeats these particulars and extends them further, attributing temper to the fog, patience to the rocks, and something close to intention to the sea. Frequency of the embellished entries does not decline with age, as one might expect of a waning enthusiasm; if anything the later pages carry the longer flights. I measured a sample: across two hundred nights, the plain record averages eleven words and the enriched record fifty-three. No external event accounts for the pattern. Storms and calm alike receive the double treatment, and a night of nothing at all is often the one most lavishly described. My provisional reading is that the man was conducting a lifelong experiment upon himself, testing what a single fact becomes when a lonely mind, night after night and with nobody at all to answer it, is allowed to turn the thing over and dress it in whatever language the dark happened to suggest. Its regularity never lapsed. The townspeople, less clinical than I, simply say he saw the coast twice over and wished to keep both.
