Nobody in the town had expected the logbook to be anything but weather. Forty years of a man alone on a rock will produce, one assumes, a record of wind and tide and little else. What the estate found, on turning the first brittle page, was stranger and far more agreeable than duty. Every entry stood twice. The keeper had written the plain fact — "Fog until noon; lamp trimmed; a gull dead on the gallery" — and beneath it, in the same careful hand, the very hour dressed in its Sunday clothes, as though the sea had feelings and meant them for him alone. Mrs. Ainsley, who had never in her life granted a bachelor the benefit of the doubt, pronounced it the confession of a romantic. The vicar thought it vanity. The schoolmistress, kinder than both, called it the only company a solitary heart could keep, which was to write itself letters and answer them.

They argued the point across three separate teas. It was settled, as such matters are settled, by nobody, and felt by everybody. A man had spent his whole life telling the truth once and loving it twice, and the town, which relished gossip far more than either, could not quite forgive him for leaving no scandal of any kind behind.
