They turned up the keeper's logbook in a sea-chest last April, and the town has been quarreling over it ever since. Forty years of nights, and every one of them set down twice. His first version is plain as a nail — wind northeast, lamp trimmed, a schooner passed at ten. Beneath it sits the very same night in its Sunday suit, where the gale becomes a wolf at the panes and the schooner a hearse freighted with the drowned. Which keeper are we to believe? The banker swears the flat lines are gospel and the whole lower half is only a bald old bachelor showing off his vocabulary before an ocean that never once stopped to applaud. Our parson, who has buried more souls than the banker has ever swindled, swears the reverse, and holds that a man confesses his true religion down in the margins, where no inspector ever thinks to look.

I sat up two entire nights over both columns by a borrowed lamp, comparing the dry man above with the wild one below until the pair of them seemed to argue across the page in a single stubborn hand. The verdict pains me. That old liar keeps far the better book, and I mean to steal his adjectives the very moment the widow turns her back.
