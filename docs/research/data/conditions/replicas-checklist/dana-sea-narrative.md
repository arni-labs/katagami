We came ashore at the point to land oil for the light, and the old keeper showed us the book he had kept since he was a young man.

Every page held the night set down twice over. First he had written what the service demanded, in a hand as square and honest as a bricklayer's wall: the wind, the glass, the lamp filled, a brig standing north by the bar. Beneath that plain row ran a second telling, looser and darker, where the fog came aboard like a boarding party and the surf worked at the rock as a man works at a grievance. I asked him why he doubled his labor so. Nothing in the regulations, he answered, told a keeper how the sea felt against his chest at three in the morning. Some nights the first line was all he could manage. Others, when the loneliness pressed hardest, the second line ran on far longer than the first and spilled clean into the margin. The ink never wavered. Reading it over, I understood that the plain column had kept him employed and the wild one had kept him company, though which of the two men he prized the more, no living reader of the book can now safely say. He is dead, and the town cannot agree which of them to bury him as.
