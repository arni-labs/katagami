An inquiry opened the moment the relief keeper reported the anomaly. Forty years of entries, and not one of them written a single time. That book had passed three inspections without a soul remarking on it. Nobody had thought to read past the ruled columns.

Each night stood recorded in parallel, a working column and a shadow of it. One column obeyed the standing order to the letter: hour, wind, visibility, the state of the light, the rig of any vessel raising the coast. Its twin took the identical facts and let them breathe, so that a rising sea was granted a mood and a passing collier a whole invented history of debts and departures and of men who never came home. Investigators first suspected two keepers had quietly shared a single post and a single name between them. Pay ledgers put that notion down. One man had held the station from appointment to death. Handwriting confirmed it beyond argument, the plain lines and the ornamented lines flowing together from the same worn nib. The commissioners closed the file without a finding, unwilling to censure a dead man for keeping the regulation log and, beneath it, a second log that answered to nobody alive. Townspeople have since claimed the volume as their own, and read the shadow column aloud each year on the anniversary of the wreck he never once described in plain words.
