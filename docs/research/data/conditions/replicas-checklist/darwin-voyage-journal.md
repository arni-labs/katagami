The logbook came to me from the lighthouse trust, and I have spent a fortnight examining its curious construction. Each entry, without a single exception across forty years, is rendered in two hands by the one writer. Its upper line records the facts a keeper is bound to record: the hour, the bearing of the wind, the state of the lamp, the vessels sighted. The lower line, in the same ink, restates that plain fact and enlarges upon it, so that a fog becomes a breathing thing and a still sea acquires a temper. I was much struck by the constancy of the habit. A man does not sustain such a discipline for four decades unless the second telling answered some need the first could not reach. Such fidelity astonishes me. Nowhere does the hand falter or the doubling lapse, not even on the night his wife is recorded, briefly and at length, as having died.

What that deeper need may have been, the record nowhere declares, and I have learned to distrust the easy explanations that offer themselves. I incline to think he was two observers lodged within one lonely body, and that neither of them would ever consent to keep silent while the other wrote.
