I put in at the town on a falling glass and stopped a week, which is how the double logbook came my way. Her father had died in the spring, and she wanted a sailor's opinion of the thing.

She spread the forty years out on the kitchen table. Every entry stood in two versions, the way a coin has two faces. Up top ran the log a keeper is paid to keep, all wind and wick and the hour a steamer showed her lights. Underneath, in the same ink, came the night told for the pleasure of telling it, and here the man let himself go — the swell breathing under the tower, the gulls hung like paper, a squall that came down the coast wearing the look of an old debt. I read a good many pages, laughed twice, and stopped once altogether. The town, I found, was split clean down the middle. Half of them called the flourishes a waste of lamp oil and public time. Others, quieter, kept borrowing the book to read the bottom lines aloud. I sailed on the next tide without settling it for them, but I know which keeper I would have shared a watch with.
