Hold this book a moment, and tell me what you see. Nothing remarkable. It is a keeper's log, salt-stained and forty years long, the kind of dull register the sea demands of every man who tends a light. Open it. Each night of those forty years appears upon the page not once but twice. The keeper writes as a servant writes to his masters — the wind at north-east, the oil burned to the very drop, a vessel clearing the reef at half past two — and, in the self-same ink, he writes the night again as a man alone speaks into the dark, until the plain wind becomes a voice and the plain lamp a small sun set against the whole black Atlantic.

Which of the two is true? Both are true. Look at this flame: it is a fact to be measured, gas and air mixed in exact proportion, and it is a lovely thing in its own right, and no honest observer is asked to choose between the fact and the beauty. The keeper had learned that much upon his lonely rock. To weigh a thing and to wonder at it are not enemies; they are the two eyes by which a man sees it whole. His neighbours called him mad; thorough is the fitter word.
