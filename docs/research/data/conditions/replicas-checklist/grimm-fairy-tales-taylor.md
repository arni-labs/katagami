In a town by the grey northern sea there once lived a keeper who tended the great lamp upon the rocks, and he tended it faithfully for forty long years until the very day he died. When the townsfolk climbed the winding stair to fetch his body down, they found his logbook lying open upon the table, and what they read in it puzzled the wisest heads among them. For every night that he had watched, the old man had written not one account but two: the one in plain and honest words, telling only of the wind and the oil and the ships that passed; the other in language so rich and strange that the sea seemed to speak aloud and the lamp to breathe. Some said he had been bewitched by his loneliness. Others swore a spirit of the deep had guided his hand in the small hours, when no living soul was near to see him.

The parson declared it vanity and quietly closed the book. But an old woman who had known him as a boy only smiled, and said that a man who lives alone with the wind must learn somehow to keep himself company, and that the keeper had done no more than tell the truth once for his masters and once, more kindly, for his own tired heart, and then they buried him at last.
