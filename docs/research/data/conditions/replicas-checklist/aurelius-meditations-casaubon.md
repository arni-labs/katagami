Consider the keeper and his book. Forty years he watched a light, and forty years he wrote the night down twice — once as it plainly was, once as it moved him. Do not be quick to name this folly. A man set apart upon a rock, with the ocean for his only neighbour and the dark for his one companion, learns what the crowded town forgets: that the same hour is at once a plain thing and a wondrous one, and that to see it whole he must record it whole. The bare account is true. The adorned account is no less true. Nature herself writes in both hands, handing you the mere fact of the wind and the terror of it together in a single gust. Why should a man not answer her in kind? Remember this when your own days seem only to be endured. Set down what happened, honestly, sparing yourself no dullness and no defeat. Add what it meant, for meaning is not a lie you tell but a debt you owe the hour that carried you.

The town thinks him mad. You know better. He was a man who would not let a single night of his life pass unwitnessed by the two things in him still awake — the patient clerk and the grieving soul.
