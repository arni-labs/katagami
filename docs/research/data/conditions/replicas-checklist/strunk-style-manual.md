A keeper tended one light for forty years. He wrote each night twice. One version states the fact: wind north-east, oil low, a schooner clearing the reef at two. Its twin dresses the very scene in feeling — the gale an argument, the flame a small defiance, the vanishing ship a rescued soul. Both entries survive. The town cannot decide which man wrote them, the clerk or the dreamer, and quarrels pleasantly over its tea. That riddle misses the lesson.

Prose owes two duties, and he honoured each without cheating the other. State your case cleanly. Name the hour, the weather, and the passing vessel; spare your patient reader the adjectives he never once asked to meet. Adorn on purpose, and only on your own page, where extravagance harms nobody and answers to the heart alone. Never smuggle ornament into a passage meant for use. Never starve a private line that longs to sing. He kept these labours apart through four cold decades and confused them not once. Call that discipline, not lunacy. Vigorous writing divides exactly as he divided — the true from the lovely, the useful from the felt — each given its own rightful column, and never allowed to spoil the honest work of its neighbour.
