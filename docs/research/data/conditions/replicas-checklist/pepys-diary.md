Up betimes and to the estate office, being sent for about the old keeper's book that all the town now speaks of. Forty years he kept the light, and forty years he wrote his log, but the marvel of it — which I did not credit till I held the thing myself — is that he set down every single night in two hands, the one bare and true, the other got up in fine language as though he wrote for the playhouse and not for the Trinity House. Lord, how strange a man. His plain line would say only that the wind blew hard from the west and a ship passed safe at two in the morning; and under that very line, in the same ink, he would call the ship a "wandering soul carried home upon black water," which did make me laugh and, a moment after, did not. Mightily taken with it. Some at the office hold him to have been touched in his wits toward the last, poor wretch, alone so long with nothing but the sea for his gossip.

I am not of their mind. My own thought is that he found the one way a lonely man may be two at once — the clerk who reports the night, and the poet who forgives it. Home late, and could not put the strange book out of my head.
