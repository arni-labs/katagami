The town is uncommonly proud of its lighthouse keeper, who is forty years dead, and it will not let a stranger rest until he has been up to see the famous logbook. We were told it was the most truthful record on the whole coast, four thousand nights set down without a single embellishment, and we climbed the hundred and thirty steps in a spirit of reverence to behold this monument of plain honesty. It develops that every night in the book is written twice. Once the keeper put down the weather as it was, in six flat words, and directly underneath he put it down again as it ought to have been, with the sea roaring and the lamp defying the powers of darkness and the drowned crying out for their mothers. The plain version and the poem stand in two neat columns for forty years. The town shows you the plain column and swears the man never told a lie. It does not mention the other column, which is three times as long and would sink any court in the land. I confess I liked the poems better. They were untrue in every particular, and I have never in my life read anything I believed less or enjoyed more, which is generally the case with monuments, and with the people who guard them, and with me.
