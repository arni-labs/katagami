Here upon the table I have placed a book — nothing rarer than an old keeper's logbook, kept at the lighthouse yonder for forty years together. Take it in your hands and turn the leaves. What do you find? You find that every single day has been written down not once but twice: here, in the left column, the plain fact of the weather, the wind, the ships that passed; and here, on the right, the very same day told over again, but dressed in such colours that the fog becomes a silver sea and a passing gull a messenger. Now, why should a man do this? Let us not guess at it, but look. See how the plain column never changes its hand, steady and small, the work of duty; and see how the right-hand column runs larger and freer, as a man breathes out after his labour is done. I hold the two before you, side by side, that you may observe a thing we all carry within us. The keeper has done upon paper what every one of us does in the quiet of the mind: he has lived the day once, and then lived it again the way he wished it. And there, in a plain book from a plain tower, is the whole history of the human heart laid open.
