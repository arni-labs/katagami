Up betimes, and to the church tower with the churchwarden and two others, to look into the business of the old keeper's logbook, he being dead now these three weeks and much talk in the town about it. Strange to find the man had writ every day twice over, once plain as the weather stood, and once dressed up mighty fine, as though the fog were a great curtain and the sea full of wonders, which methinks it never was in my time. The churchwarden would have the book burned, but I spoke against it, and we agreed to keep it at the vestry for the present. Thence to the Antelope, where I dined on oysters and a pint of sack, and paid 1s. 8d. There I fell to reading more of the second column, which for all its lies pleased me strangely, and I confess I liked the dressed words better than the true, and was ashamed of it. Home in the dark, my head full of the dead man and his two hands, and musing whether I do not the very same in my own accounts, making the day read better at night than it felt at noon. My wife merry, and a little supper of cold fowl. So to bed, but slept ill, thinking on it.
