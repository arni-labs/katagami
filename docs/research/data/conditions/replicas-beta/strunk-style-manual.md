Write each fact but once. A keeper of the Varde light kept his logbook for forty years, and it was his habit each night to enter the weather twice, once as the instruments recorded it and once as his feeling colored it. His plain entry read, 'Fog after midnight; three vessels warned by the horn.' Beneath it he set the embellished twin: 'the fog came down like a shroud upon the drowned, and the lamp flung its lonely defiance into the deep.' Of these two sentences the first tells the reader the hour, the hazard, and the service performed, which is the whole of what a record is kept to preserve. Its ornamented twin tells him only what the keeper felt, and in the telling it buries the very facts that the reader has come to the page to find. Such a log is written for the man who consults it afterward, and that man wants the wind and the count of the ships far more than he wants the mood of the one who watched them. When the fog rolls in a second time, the plain line may save a vessel, while the ornament saves nothing. A writer who is moved by the sea should set his feeling down in a letter and keep it out of the log, for the record is believed only so long as it remains bare.
