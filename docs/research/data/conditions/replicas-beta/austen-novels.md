It was long the opinion of Barrow-on-Sands that a man who kept a faithful record of his own dullness had done the town a kindness. The late keeper of the light had kept such a record for forty years, and was thought to have been kind indeed. That he had written every entry twice — once as the weather was, and once as he wished it had been — was not discovered until his death. The two hands, the plain and the embellished, ran side by side down every page. Plain folk declared themselves shocked. The vainer sort were secretly delighted. Both agreed, with that warmth which conceals a want of feeling, that they had always suspected him of something. Mrs. Carr, who had never read a page in her life, was loudest in lamenting that so honest a man should prove so double. The vicar preferred the embellished column, finding there a poetry the fogs of forty years had otherwise withheld from him. It was at length concluded that the keeper had been lonely — an explanation which cost the speakers nothing, and relieved them of the trouble of thinking him strange; for a town will forgive a man any oddity, provided it may first pity him for it, and afterwards forget that it was ever surprised.
