There was once an old lighthouse keeper who tended the great lamp upon the black rocks for forty years, and never in all that time did a single ship go down while his light was burning. Every night he wrote in his book what the sea had done, and every night he wrote it again a second time, but the second telling was not like the first. The first was plain and true: the wind blew hard from the north, and two boats came safe to harbour. The second was grand and golden: the storm rode up like a black horse out of the deep, and the little boats crept home beneath the keeper's faithful star. So it went for forty years, one hand plain and one hand shining, until the old man died and the townsfolk climbed the stair to fetch his book. When they read the two tellings side by side, they did not know which to believe, and they quarrelled the whole day long. Now the fisherfolk said the plain words were the true keeper. The children said the golden words, and would hear no other. Then the mayor shut the book and set it in the church, where both tellings might be kept, and where each man who came might choose the keeper he liked best. And so it rests there to this day.
