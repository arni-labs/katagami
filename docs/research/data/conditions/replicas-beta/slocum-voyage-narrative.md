I put in below the light on the third of October, the wind having hauled to the northeast, and made the Nell fast to the old stone pier under the tower. Its keeper had been dead a month. It fell to me, as the first man up from the water in a while, to see to his books, and I carried them down to the fire. There were forty years in that pile. One volume to the year, and every day set down twice over. The plain entry came first: wind, glass, the hour he lit the lamp, the hour he put it out. Below that he had written the same day again, but dressed, the fog made into a living thing, the sea given a temper it does not own. A man alone will talk to himself, and I saw no harm in it. I read till the lamp wanted oil, and I own the finer entries were the better reading, though the plain ones were the ones I trusted. The town, when I told them, could not agree what to make of it. Some held he had gone strange in his solitude; others that he was a poet the whole coast had missed. I thought he had simply kept two logs because one was true and the other was his, and both were honest work. The Nell lay quiet at the pier through all of it, and in the morning I took the books ashore to the parish and went back down to her.
