Consider the keeper who wrote his nights down twice. Forty winters he tended the flame, and forty winters he set beside the plain record a second and a gilded one, as though the sea, being bare, were not enough, unless he made it grander than it was. Ask thyself wherefore. A thing beheld truly hath no need of ornament; yet the man could not endure to leave it bare, and so he clothed each fog and each wreck in borrowed light, that some reader, coming after, might deem him greater than his office. This is vanity. Thou dost the like, when thou tellest thy own day over unto thyself in fairer words than it did merit. Mark it, and cease. What is a storm, that thou shouldst embellish it? What is thy labour, that it should be sung? Do thou the work; write it plain; let the next man judge. The town, finding two hands within one book, quarrelled which was the true keeper — the honest clerk, or the poet. They are one man, and both are dust. Praise not the one, neither pity thou the other. Keep the plain entry within thine own breast, and burn the gilded leaf ere it flatter thee. The Gods asked of him light, and not eloquence. They ask no more of thee.
