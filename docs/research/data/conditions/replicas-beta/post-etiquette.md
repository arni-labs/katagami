When a man has kept the light for forty years and written his logbook twice each night — once as the weather was, and once as he dreamed it — the town that finds him out owes him a certain delicacy. He is dead and cannot explain himself, and the dead are the one company we may never contradict. To read his private embellishments aloud in the tavern, as some did, and to laugh at the silver he gave to a grey sea, is not wit but a want of breeding. A considerate reader remembers that the second column was written for no eyes but the keeper's own.

The custom, in such a case, is to praise the faithful column and to be silent about the other. One may admire the poetry without publishing the man's loneliness to the parish.

There is, of course, always a Mrs. Sharp, who declares she "never trusted him" and reads out his finest passages in a mincing voice for the amusement of her callers. She mistakes ridicule for discernment, which are not the same thing at all. The exception is the scholar who studies the book with respect, for learning honestly pursued excuses much. A person of real feeling closes the ledger gently, keeps the keeper's dreams to himself, and lets a lonely man have written whatever kept him warm.
