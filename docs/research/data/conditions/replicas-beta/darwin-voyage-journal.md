August 3rd.—The old lighthouse stands upon a spit of granite, and I passed the morning in its upper room examining the keeper's logbook, which extends over forty years and fills nine leather volumes. I was at once struck by a singular circumstance. Every entry has been written twice: first in a short, plain hand recording the wind, the tide, and the vessels sighted; and below it, in a fuller hand, the same night set down again with much elaboration and figurative language. I counted the entries of one month, and of thirty-one nights not one wanted its second version. The two hands are plainly the same man's, though the ornamented one is larger and more careful, as if made at leisure. It resembles, in this doubling, the practice of certain naturalists who keep a rough field-book and afterwards copy it fair; yet here the fair copy adds what the field-book never held. Why the keeper did so, whether for his own pleasure or for some reader he imagined, I am unable to determine. The townspeople hold the two versions in curious esteem. Some read only the plain columns and call him honest; others prefer the embellished, and call him a poet of the coast. The point admits of no decision from the book alone, and I left it unresolved, as I found it.
