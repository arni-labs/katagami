Dear Sir,—You desire to know what our parish has made of the strange logbook lately found in the old lighthouse upon the point, and I will gladly send you the little I have gathered, though it belongs more to the study of men than to that of birds. The keeper who kept it is now forty years dead, and through the whole of his long service he wrote down each night not once but twice. His first entry was short and exact, giving the wind, the state of the tide, and the vessels that passed; the second was longer and much adorned, wherein the same plain night is dressed in storm and glory, as a common thrush is sometimes described by writers who have never once watched one feed. I have compared a great many pairs of these entries, and I am satisfied that the plain hand was set down first and at the moment, and the ornamented hand afterward and at leisure; for the fuller ink sits always upon the shorter, and never the shorter upon the fuller. Our parish is much divided upon the matter. The fishermen esteem the short entries and call the man honest, while the younger sort prefer the embellished and would have him a poet of the coast. Which telling stood nearer his true mind I will not venture to pronounce, having observed too often that the plainest watcher is also the fondest dreamer, and that the two may lodge in one breast without any quarrel. I remain, dear Sir, your obliged servant.
