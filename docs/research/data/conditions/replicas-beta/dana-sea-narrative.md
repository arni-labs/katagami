The keeper died in the spring, and it fell to three of us to clear the light and enter what he left. Forty logbooks lay stacked by year, bound in oilcloth against the damp, and we read them through by the lamp we had climbed up to tend. Each day was written twice. First came the plain hand — wind sou'-west, glass falling, the lamp trimmed at dusk and drawing well — and beneath it the same day told over in a finer script, the gale given teeth, the fog laid on the water like a sheet over the drowned. "He kept two watches," said Elias, and laughed, though none of us joined him. Reading held us there until the oil ran low. There is something in a man who sets down his own weather twice, once for the record and once for himself, that a sailor knows and does not care to say aloud. Afterward the town made a great matter of it, and the parson would have the finer passages read out from the pulpit. Those of us who had stood the reading kept our peace. His plain entries were true, and the finer ones were enough. We stacked the books as we had found them, put out the lamp, and went down to the boat in the grey of the morning.
