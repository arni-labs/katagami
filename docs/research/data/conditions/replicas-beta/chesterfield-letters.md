Dear boy, — You will have heard by now of the old lighthouse-keeper lately dead, whose logbook, kept forty years, is found to carry every day twice written: once as it was, and once as he would have had it. Half the town calls him a liar and half a poet, and you may learn a great deal from standing a little apart from both. A man who tells the truth to his ledger and a fine tale to himself is not therefore a hypocrite; he is only human, and you will do the same, whether you own it or not. The thing to master is not the second telling, for that we cannot help, but the knowing of which column is which. Keep, then, two reckonings of your own days, but never confuse them. Let the plain one govern your conduct and your accounts, where a shilling miscounted is a shilling lost; let the other console you in the dark, where it does no harm. The fault is not in dreaming a better day, but in acting upon the dream as though it were the fact. Observe this week how often you tell yourself the flattering column and call it the true one. I shall write next of the keeping of accounts, which asks a colder eye still. Adieu.
