For a proper record, if the facts alone are wanted, the facts alone should be given; and it was long the rule among keepers that a log was no place for fine writing. But the late keeper of our own light thought otherwise, and beside every plain entry set a second, embroidered one, until his forty years' book became two books in one—the one a witness, the other very nearly a romance. The town, naturally, is divided. The plain people read the plain column and are satisfied; the rest turn straight to the flourishes and will hear of nothing else. A late leader of our little society, famous for reading aloud after dinner, would open the log only at its grandest passages and give them out like scripture. "The man was a poet on a rock--never mind the weather he actually had!" is what she used to say, and her guests, of course, applauded, and no one dared ask what the plain column said. That was extreme; but, even so, better than the other party, who would tear the fine half out altogether and keep only the bald one. The truth of the matter is that both halves were his, written on the same night by the same tired hand, and it is neither good manners nor good sense to honour the one and be ashamed of the other.
