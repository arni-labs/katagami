612. THE KEEPER'S LOG, of which we here give some account, together with the manner in which it was kept, was discovered upon the death of the late light-keeper, after forty years of service. This consists of a single great volume, ruled down the middle, the left half reserved for the plain record of each night, and the right for a fuller and more ornamented account of the same. The plain half was required of him by the Trinity board, and should on no account be neglected; for there can be no trustworthy record without plain entry, and "Spare the plain word, and spoil the log" might very fairly be set beside the older proverb. The ornamented half was of his own devising, and answered, it seems, to the tedium of the long watches.

613. IN KEEPING SUCH A RECORD, the plain column should always be written first, and while the facts are fresh, so that there be no danger of the finer phrases creeping in among them and colouring what ought to stand bare.

614. IT WILL BE FOUND PREFERABLE, where a log is meant for use and not for show, to keep the two hands well apart; for a record in which fact and fancy are mingled is like a dish over-sauced,—the true flavour is lost, and cannot afterward be recovered.
