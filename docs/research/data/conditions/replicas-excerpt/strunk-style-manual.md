The two columns of the keeper's log illustrate, more clearly than any precept could, the difference between recording a thing and dressing it up. The plain entry states what happened. The embellished entry states what happened and then, at considerable length, states it again.

A common fault is to suppose that the longer account is the fuller one. It is not. "Wind high; light kept; no wreck" tells the reader everything he needs; the paragraph of drowned moons and shrieking gulls that follows it tells him only about the writer.

This is not to condemn the second column outright. A man alone on a rock may write as finely as he pleases, and the finest passages here are very fine indeed. But when fact and ornament share a page, the reader's trust goes to the plainer neighbour, and the ornament, for all its labour, is discounted.

Prefer the plain entry. It is not that plainness is a virtue in itself, but that the reader, consciously or unconsciously, is dissatisfied with being told how a night felt when he has not yet been told what the night was. Tell him what the night was. The feeling, if it is worth anything, will reach him without a single shrieking gull.
