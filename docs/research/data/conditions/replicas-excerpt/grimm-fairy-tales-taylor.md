Once upon a time there was an old keeper of a lighthouse who had tended his lamp for forty years, and every night, when the light was burning, he wrote in a great book what had passed. But he wrote each night twice: once plainly, just as it was, and once in fine and glittering words, as though the sea were a kingdom and he its only knight. When at last the old man died, the townsfolk climbed the winding stair and found the book lying open, and the two writings began to speak. "Read me, read me," cried the plain words, "for I am true, and every wave I name did truly break." "Nay, read me," cried the fine words, "for I am beautiful, and I make the cold nights warm." The people could not agree, and quarrelled all the way down the stair. Some said the plain book should go to the harbour office, to be believed; and some said the fine book should be read aloud at the inn, to be loved. And in the end the wise old woman of the town, who had such large kind eyes, said, 'Let neither be torn from the other, for the man wrote both with the same hand, on the same nights, by the same small flame.' So they bound the two halves together for ever, and set the book where all might read, and were content.
