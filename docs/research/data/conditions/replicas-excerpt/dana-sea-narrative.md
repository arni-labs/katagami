The old keeper having died, and the light passing to a younger man, we were sent up to the tower to clear out what he had left. Thus ended the forty years' watch of as faithful a man as ever trimmed a wick. We found the place bare enough, but for the logbooks, which stood in a long row upon a shelf, well thumbed and salt-stained, and heavier than we looked for. It was in turning these over, of an evening, that we first came upon the strange thing: every night set down twice, once in a few plain words, and once at length, with all manner of fine description laid on. For two days we did nothing but read, from the grey of the morning until the lamp wanted lighting, turning the leaves and reading aloud to one another. For keeping such a record, that rock was as lonely a station as any on the coast. There was no near neighbour; no boat came but once a fortnight; and the fogs shut a man in for weeks together. For these reasons, we judged, the keeper had taken to writing twice, to make some company out of pen and ink; and, indeed, it would be hard for any man to pass so many winters alone, and not fall into some such shift to keep his mind whole.
