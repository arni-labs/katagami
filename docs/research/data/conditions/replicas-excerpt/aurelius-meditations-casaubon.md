XLI. Within a very little while the keeper is ashes, and his lamp another man's charge, and his great book a curiosity for idle hands to turn over of an afternoon. And what are these two columns of his, over which the town now wrangles, but one vanity laid beside another? The plain, which saith the wind blew and the light was kept; and the fine, which saith the same, but with such swelling of words as the poets use. Consider both, and thou shalt find them, if rightly esteemed, but as the babbling of children who tell a small thing over and over that it may seem great. For the night that he watched is gone, whether he wrote of it barely or gloriously; and he himself is gone after it. What then remains that is worth a wise man's keeping? Not the flourish, surely, and scarcely the record. Only this: that a man set at a hard post should tend his light, and speak the truth of what he sees, and neither despise the long dark nor adorn it with lies to make it bear. The rest—the fair phrases, the reputation, the quarrel of the town over his memory—is smoke, and a rebounding echo, and belongs neither to him, nor to thee.
