To return now to the keeper's book itself. See here what a curious thing lies open upon the table! You observe, there are two entries set against every single night, the one short and the other long. And now, as we cannot doubt that both were written by the same hand, and on the same evening, we may fairly ask what the second gives us that the first did not. Take the night of the great gale. In the plain column he has put only, "Wind north-east, hard; light kept; no wreck." I have here the very leaf, and you shall see how the same fact, in the fuller column, is dressed about with black water and shrieking gulls and a moon like a drowned lantern. [The Lecturer held the page toward the company.] Now mark this well. The one thing that does not change, through all this finery, is the fact. The wind was north-east; the light was kept; no ship was lost. Pour away the ornament, as we pour cold water upon the steam, and the fact does not collapse—it stands. I shew you this for the purpose of pointing out that in all these double entries there is nothing that turns the night into another night. It remains the night it was; and the embellishment, however it swells and glitters, adds not one grain to what truly happened there upon the rock.
