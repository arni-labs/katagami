Since the affair of the keeper's double log is now the talk of every table in the town, and since you will assuredly be asked what you make of it, I will give you my own opinion, that you may not be caught with a foolish one of your own. The plain entries, you are to understand, were his duty, and the ornamented ones his diversion; and I find no great harm in a man's amusing his solitude, provided he never mistakes the one column for the other. But I do desire that you will take this lesson from him: keep your accounts of the world exact, and your embellishments to yourself. A man who writes finely of a calm night, till his reader believes a tempest raged, has told a lie, however prettily; and the prettiness is precisely what makes it dangerous. Learn to see plainly first; you will have time enough afterwards to see beautifully. The keeper, I dare say, did his gazing honestly, and only warmed the record when the lamp was lit and the night long. That is pardonable. What is not pardonable is to live so much in the fine column that you forget which things were real. Be, in your dealings and your letters, 'exact avant tout'; the graces may follow, but they must never lead.
