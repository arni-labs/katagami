How quaint, and homely, and altogether human this old ledger seemed, until we understood what manner of double-dealing lay hid between its covers! Think of a plain man—an unlettered keeper of a lamp upon a rock—setting down each night's weather twice: once as God sent it, and once as he wished the angels to remember it!

The first hand was honest as a boot: "Wind SW, fog till noon, oiled the wick." But underneath, in a finer and more secret script, the same night bloomed into glory: the fog became "a grey host besieging the tower," the wick "a small sun kindled against the drowning dark." Forty years of it! Two thousand pages of weather, each entry a lie and a truth married in the selfsame breath.

What, more? The townspeople came in a body to see it, and stood about the table in a hush, as though a coffin lay there. One old woman wept. The parson pronounced it a sermon in disguise; the schoolmaster called it fraud; and a boy of nine asked, with the terrible simplicity of boys, which one the keeper had believed. Nobody could answer him. They carried the book to the town hall and set it under glass, that reverent pilgrims might file past and admire—the plain truth on top, and beneath it, forever, the truth the poor man could not help but tell!
