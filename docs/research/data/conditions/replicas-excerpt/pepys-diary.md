14th. At the quay good part of the morning about the herring tallies, and then toward noon with my wife on foot up to the point to see the light-house, the old keeper being now three weeks dead and the whole town in a stir about his books. My wife went in to sit with the widow above, and I stayed below in the little room talking with Mr. Prall the clerk, turning over the great logbook that lies there, and finding to my wonder that every night is writ down twice—once plain, as "wind hard, ship passed, lamp trimmed," and once again in a fine hand, with such flourishes of the sea and the stars as put me into a strange suspense of mirth and pity both. And while I read, comes word that the parson is below and would have the book for the vestry, which vexed Mr. Prall exceedingly, he saying the family should keep it. He very hot upon this, and I saying little. So to our dinner at the inn, cold beef and a good lobster, and my wife merry. After dinner to the churchyard to see the new-cut stone, and thence, it beginning to rain, my wife and I slipped away to hear a blind man sing ballads under the arch, which was the first time, and indeed very pleasant; but so poor a voice I never heard I think in my life. And so home, and to bed.
