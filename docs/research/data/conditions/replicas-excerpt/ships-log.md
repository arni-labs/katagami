The books being got down to the boat, we pulled off to the schooner and stowed them in the mate's cabin. Thus we carried away the whole labour of a man's life in two sea-chests. We were curious over them, but tired, and more willing to turn in than to read; so we laid them by till the passage down. It seems the mate had been ashore and heard the story from the townspeople, who were full of it, and could talk of nothing else; and each of them had his own reading of the matter, and none would give up his to another. Some held the keeper a poet cheated of his calling; some, that he was touched in the head; and the older sort, who had known him, only shook their heads and said he was a quiet man and did his duty. For settling such a question there is no worse place than a small town, where every man knows the next, and will not be put down. We, for our part, took no side, having work of our own; but at night, in the watch below, we read the doubled entries by the swinging lamp, and owned among ourselves that the second hand, for all its flourish, told the truer weather of the two.
