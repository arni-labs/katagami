On examining the keeper's logbook, which had been kept without interruption for forty years, I was surprised to find that every entry had been written twice over: once in a plain and hurried hand, and once in a fuller manner, much embellished. At first I supposed that the second version had been added long afterward, and perhaps by another person; but upon comparing the ink and the wear of successive pages, it appeared to me most probable that both had been set down on the same evening, and by the same hand. Out of some fourteen thousand entries which I examined, not one wanted its double. The plain record noted only the wind, the state of the sea, and the hours the lamp was burning; the second dwelt upon the colour of the water, the cries of birds, and the keeper's own reflections. On any supposition it is a curious circumstance that a man should trouble to describe, in solitude and to no reader, the same night in two so different manners. There are a few accounts of such doubled journals among the lightkeepers of that coast. The cause is probably to be sought in the great want of society, so that a man long alone, having recorded what his office required, would be very apt to write again for his own company.
