I put in under the light at dusk, the wind failing me within a mile of the rock, and made fast at the old landing where the keepers of forty years had made fast before me. The new man met me at the door and was glad enough of a face; for, to tell the truth, the station had told on him, and he had not spoken to a soul in nineteen days. He showed me the dead keeper's books by way of welcome. "Read there," he said, and set the lamp between us. I read, and was fairly brought up standing. Every night of forty years stood written twice: once bare, as a log should be, and once dressed out in its Sunday clothes, with the sea "marching grey upon the tower" and the fog "a hand laid over the eye of the light." I said, "The man wrote for two, then, being one." The keeper nodded. Now, I was not for letting the book go to the damp and the mice, and so I told him to carry it down to the town and put it where folk could see it. He did, and the town has made a small shrine of it since, and charges a penny to look. The dead keeper, I dare say, would have written that up handsomely, and charged nothing.
