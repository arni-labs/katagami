"You must allow me, madam," said Mr. Featherstone, with the warmth of a man persuaded of his own consequence, "to congratulate the parish upon a discovery of the first importance; for it is not every age that is favoured with a document at once so voluminous and so improving. That the late keeper should have set down each night twice over—once as it plainly was, and once as it might with propriety have been—argues a delicacy of feeling the common run of men would do well to imitate; and I do not scruple to affirm that the embellished column alone would furnish out a handsome volume for the subscription library."

"Really, Mr. Featherstone," cried Mrs. Hale, with some warmth, "you astonish me. If a man writes the fact in one column and a fable in the next, I know not by what rule we are bound to prefer the fable, merely because it is the finer."

"You must give me leave to flatter myself, my dear madam, that your objection is a mere form of words. It does not appear to me that a plain account of a plain night can be bettered by the addition of moonlight the keeper never saw; and the finer a thing is written, the more it stands in want of believing."

"I do assure you, sir, that I have no pretensions whatever to that kind of taste which consists in admiring a man for what he did not do. I would rather be told what the weather was."
