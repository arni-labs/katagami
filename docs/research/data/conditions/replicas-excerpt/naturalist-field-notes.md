On the third day, when going through the later volumes, I paid particular attention to the manner in which the two hands diverged. In the earlier years the embellished entry exceeded the plain one by perhaps a third; but towards the end it had grown to three and even four times its length, until a single night's weather filled the better part of a page. I was at first inclined to attribute this to failing eyesight, which enlarges the hand of old men; yet the plain entries remained small and even throughout, and only the second swelled. It appeared to me, therefore, more probable that the habit had strengthened with the solitude, as a plant long kept from the light will draw itself out toward the window. Of the whole forty years I could find but two nights left undoubled, both in the winter of a year marked by a heavy storm, when, I imagine, the keeper had leisure only for the necessary record. Mr. Colenso, who has examined a similar book from a northern station, remarks upon the same increase. The circumstance is the more interesting because no one required the second writing, nor, so far as can be learned, was it ever shown to a living soul during the writer's life.
