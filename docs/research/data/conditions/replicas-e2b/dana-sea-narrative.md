The keeper of the light on the point had stood his watch forty years, and when he died the town went up to the house to see what a man leaves who tends a lamp and speaks to no one, and found the logs stacked to the rafters, every year of them bound in oilcloth against the damp. A landsman would have opened one and read the weather and shut it again; but the schoolmaster, turning the pages, saw that each entry had been set down twice — once in the short hand a man keeps who must be brief because the wick wants trimming, and once below it in a fuller hand, the same night written over as though the keeper had gone back after the lamp was lit and the sea had quieted, and given himself leave to say what the first line would not. Wind NW, the first would run, hard, ship stood off the bar. Then under it: the gale came down the coast like a thing that had been sent, and the running lights of a collier heeled and steadied and held, and a man thought of the hands aloft in it. The town argued a week over which line was the true one. The sailors among them did not argue. They knew a man keeps two logs, and which one he keeps for himself.
