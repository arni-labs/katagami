Write with one voice.

The keeper of the Fenwick light kept his log for forty years. Every entry he wrote twice: once plain, once dressed. The plain entry served. The dressed one wasted the reader's time and his own.

Prefer the plain record.

A logbook reports weather, ships, and the state of the lamp. It has one duty. The keeper wrote, "Wind NW, gale by dark, lamp trimmed twice." That entry does its work. He wrote also, "The wind came out of the northwest like a wolf loosed upon the water, and the great lamp stood against it." The second says no more than the first. It says it slower.

Omit the ornament.

Wrong: "The sea rose in fury and the rocks drank the drowning light."
Right: "Sea heavy. Two lights lost off the point."

The town read both columns and argued which was the true man. They were both the true man. One column told what happened. The other told how he felt about it, and feeling, in a logbook, is a needless word.

Keep the record; strike the flourish.

Forty years, two columns, one keeper. The plain column can be read in an afternoon. The other cannot be read at all, only admired, and a log is not written to be admired.
