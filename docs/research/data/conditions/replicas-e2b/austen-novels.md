It was generally agreed in the town, that a man who keeps a light burning for forty years has thereby earned the right to be thought devoted; and when Mr. Aldous Renn was found, after his death, to have written each night's account twice over—once as it had happened, and once as he should have preferred it to happen—the town was not so much scandalised as gratified, having always suspected there was more in him than the plain man had let anyone see. The vicar pronounced the embellished entries a species of poetry, and read three of them aloud, with feeling. The schoolmistress, who had once been refused a dance by the keeper's brother, observed only that a man with so much leisure for adjectives can have had very little for his lamp. Mrs. Carew, who prided herself upon discernment, declared the plain entries the truer record and the fine ones the truer man, and was much admired for the distinction, though no one could afterwards say precisely what it meant. It was left to the keeper's own daughter to settle the matter. She had read both columns since girlhood, she said, and had never once found them to disagree upon the weather. Whether this was offered in her father's defence or against it she did not explain; and the town, unwilling to be robbed of so agreeable a mystery, was too well-bred to press her.
