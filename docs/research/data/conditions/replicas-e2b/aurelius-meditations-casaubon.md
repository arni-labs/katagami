Consider the keeper of the light, who for forty years set down each night's labour twice: once as it was, once as he wished it remembered. The town, finding this, disputed which hand was the honest one. Trouble not thyself with their dispute. Ask rather which hand thou wouldst be caught writing with.

The plain entry is the work. The wind blew, the lamp was tended, a ship passed safe: this is enough, for nature asked no more of him, and duty asks no more of thee. The second entry, the dressed one, he wrote for a reader he would never meet. So dost thou also, when thou labourest with one eye upon the labour and one upon the report of it.

Do thy office and be done. The sea does not embellish its rising, nor the rock its standing. They are what they are, and are sufficient. Be thou likewise sufficient. When thou hast trimmed the wick, thou hast trimmed the wick; add nothing.

He is dead now, this keeper, and both his columns with him, and the town keeps his book behind glass and cannot agree. This is the end of the man who writes for men. Write for the thing itself, and let no one, not even thyself, come after to admire it.
