March 3rd.—Examined the keeper's logs, forty volumes, removed from the light on the point after his death and laid out in the vestry, where the window gave good north light. The books are of the common ledger sort, foolscap, bound in oilcloth, and number one to the year of his service, which the parish register fixes at forty. On opening them I found what the sexton had reported: every daily entry is written twice. The upper entry is brief — wind, sea, the hours of lighting and drawing the lamp — in a small upright hand. Below it, in the same ink and the same hand but larger, stands a second entry, two or three times the length, giving the weather not as a figure but as a scene. I counted the doubled entries in the volume for 1874 and found 361 of the 365 so written; the four omitted fall within a week the parish notes he lay ill. I would not venture to say why a solitary man should keep his record twice over, once for the record and once, as it appears, for no reader at all. The town supposes vanity, or the long want of company. Comparison offers little: the ships' logs I have seen are single, and terse to the last. I set down here only what the volumes show, and leave the cause where I found it, unexplained.
