Dear Sir,

You ask what has become of the keeper's book at the Fenwick light, of which you had some report in town; and as the matter has furnished our parish with talk enough for a whole wet spring, I am glad to send you what I have gathered on the spot.

The keeper, who died in the autumn past after tending the light some forty years, kept a nightly register, as all such men are bound to do. But he did a thing besides that his office nowhere required: he wrote each entry a second time, in the same book and on the same page, dressing the plain fact in a fuller and more ornamented style, so that the wind which in one line merely "backed to the south" was in the next made to sigh and labour like a living creature.

Our vicar will have it that the man grew disordered in his long solitude; but this I cannot allow, for the hand is even and the dates unbroken to the last, which is not the manner of a disordered mind. I incline rather to think he kept the one column for his masters and the other for the winter nights, which on that rock are very long. Whether he ever meant the second to be read, I will not pretend to say, the question being one his book does not answer.

I am, Sir, your obedient servant.
