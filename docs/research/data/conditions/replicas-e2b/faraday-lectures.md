Here upon the table before you I have placed a very ordinary thing: a keeper's logbook, one of forty, in which a man wrote down the doings of his lighthouse every night for the whole length of a working life. You would suppose there was nothing in it but wind and weather. But look closely, and you will see that every single day has been written not once, but twice. Now why should a tired man take such trouble? Let us not guess at it; let us look. Here is the first entry—see how plain it is: the hour, the state of the sea, the oil consumed. And here, directly beneath, is that same day over again, only now the sea has been given a voice, and the fog a temper, and the light itself is spoken of as a living watcher upon the rocks. Hold the page up to this lamp. You will find the plain words pressed hard into the paper, and the fine words written light, as though in a gentler hour of the same night. What does this teach us? That one man carried two ways of seeing, and would surrender neither. You and I do very much the same, every day of our lives, though few of us keep both columns. The town asks which record is the true one. I would answer you: hold the two of them up together to the light, and there you have the man entire.
