The guidebook devotes a page and a half to the celebrated logbook of the Fenwick light, and calls it "the twin diary of a soul divided between the actual and the sublime." We paid a shilling to see it and were shown into a small damp room where the book lies open under glass that has not been wiped since the sublime was fashionable.

The keeper, it seems, wrote every entry two times. On the left he put down the weather. On the right he put down the same weather again, only this time it had been to college. "Wind fresh from the west" became, on the right, a wind that "rode down upon the deep like the breath of an offended Providence." There are, by the custodian's count, fourteen thousand six hundred such nights, which is to say twenty-nine thousand two hundred entries, of which precisely half are true.

The town cannot decide whether their keeper was a great writer or a lonely one, and has erected a plaque that carefully declines to say. I studied both columns for an hour and reached a firm conclusion, which I here set down for the benefit of the traveler: the man was bored. I know it because I had, by then, read the whole left-hand column, and had begun composing a right-hand one of my own.
