July 3rd.—Fenwick Point. Examined this morning the logbook of the late keeper, kept in the town hall and now much handled. The volume is bound in oiled canvas and comprises, by my count, some fourteen hundred leaves, closely written on both sides in a small upright hand.

The peculiarity is soon apparent. Each night's record is entered not once but twice, in two columns ruled down the page. The left column is brief: the direction of the wind, the number of vessels sighted, the hours the lamp burned. The right column treats of the same night in language of a wholly different character, elevated and figurative, such as one meets in books of poetry rather than in a register of duty.

I compared many pairs of entries. In every instance the facts of the two agree exactly; only the manner differs. The habit is sustained without a single lapse across forty winters, which I find the more remarkable, the labour being unrewarded and, so far as can be shown, unread until his death.

Of the cause I can say nothing certain. The townspeople offer that he wrote the one column for the harbour-master and the other for himself; but this I set down as their opinion, not as a thing observed. Why a man should keep two records of one sea, and show neither, remains to me unexplained.
