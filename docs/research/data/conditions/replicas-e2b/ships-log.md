Made the point light at dusk with the wind hauling to the north and a sea getting up on the bar. We logged it as the watch requires: Wind N, freshening. Heavy swell. Light bearing NNE, four miles. That is the whole of what a log wants, and for forty years that was the whole of what the keeper's log seemed to give, until the last of the volumes came ashore after he died and was found written twice over. Every entry stood in two hands. The first was the log's own: the hour, the glass, the state of the sea, the chronometer wound and checked against the noon sight. Under it ran a second entry, fuller and still dry, where the man had set down the night as he had watched it — the gale coming down the coast, the running lights of a collier heeling and holding, the lamp steady through the whole of it. The town took the fuller book for the keepsake and shelved it. The seamen among them took the plain one. They knew which log a man trusts. You do not lose your position by writing it plainly, and the keeper never lost his. Both books agreed to the hour on every night of the forty years.
