I put into the harbor under the point on the last of the flood, and the keeper came down to take my line, an old man who had kept the light forty years and, it was said, had never once let it fail. He asked after my chronometer, and I told him it was an old tin clock that kept time as well as I asked of it, which was not much. He laughed at that. When he died the next winter the town found his logs, forty years of them, and every night entered twice. The first entry was a keeper's: the wind, the sea, the hour the lamp was lit, the hour it was drawn. The second was longer, set down under the first in the same steady hand, and gave what the night had been and not only where the wind had stood. I have kept a log at sea and know the difference. A man writes the first line for whoever comes after him and the second for himself, and does not look to have it read. The town read it. They set the fuller book in the library under glass and left the plain one in a drawer. I would have kept the plain one. It was the truer of the two, and it was the one that had brought the ships in.
