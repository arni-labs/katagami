Dear boy,—You will have heard by now of the lighthouse keeper whose logbook, kept forty years, was found to hold every day written twice: once as it was, and once as he wished it to be. The town is divided, some calling him a poet and others a liar, and I would have you learn from the man and from the town alike. Observe first that he did his plain duty in the plain column, and reserved his fancy for the second; the light never once went out because he loved fine words. There is your rule for the week: indulge the imagination only after the business is finished, never before it, and never at its cost. Observe next how the town, having no such record of its own to be proud of, hurries to sit in judgment upon his. Men are always readiest to condemn in a stranger the very habit they themselves lack the discipline to keep. Be slow, therefore, to pronounce upon what any man did in private for his own comfort. And mark, above all, that he showed those finer entries to nobody; the discovery was an accident of his death. He who performs his refinements for an audience is a coxcomb; he who keeps them to himself is something better. Attempt the second sort this week, and tell me in your next letter where you succeeded and where you fell short. Adieu.
