When the private papers of the dead come unexpectedly to light, the first question is never what they contain, but whether the world has any business reading them at all. The keeper who wrote his forty years of logs in two hands--one plain, one adorned--set down the second hand for himself alone, and the accident of death having opened the book confers upon the town no right to treat his confidences as public property. Best society reads such a discovery with a lowered voice. The plain entries, being the record of a public duty, may be shown and studied without offence; they belong, in a manner of speaking, to the coast he lighted through so many nights. The embellished entries are another matter entirely, for they were the man's solitude committed to ink, and to pass them from hand to hand for the amusement of an evening is to make a spectacle of the very privacy their author guarded all his life. There is no unkindness in admiring them quietly; there is a distinct want of feeling in exhibiting them. The exception is plain enough: should the family choose to share the finer pages, that choice is theirs and no one else's to make. Those who would print such things for profit, or recite them for laughter, mistake mere possession of a thing for permission to use it--an error which best society has never once made, and never will.
