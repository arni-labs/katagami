Consider the keeper who set down his nights twice over. Forty years he tended the flame, and forty years he laid beside the plain record a second, gilded one, as though the sea were not enough unto him unless he made it grander than it was. Ask thyself wherefore. A thing seen truly needeth no ornament; yet the man could not bear to leave it bare, and so he dressed each fog and each wreck in borrowed colour, that some reader, coming after, might find him greater than his office. This is vanity. Thou dost the same, when thou tellest thy day over to thyself in fairer words than it deserveth. Mark it, and stop. What is a storm, that thou shouldst embellish it? and what thy labour, that it should be sung? and what art thou, that thy nights should be gilded above the truth of them? Do the work; write it plain; let the next man judge. The town, finding the two hands in one book, quarrelled over which was the true keeper: whether the honest clerk that set the hour down bare, or the maker of fair words who could not let it stand so. They are the same man, and both are dust. Keep thou the plain entry in thine own breast, and burn the gilded page before it flatter thee. The Gods asked of him light for the ships; the gilding they neither required nor remember. The office is still owed, and thou its keeper yet a little while, and then not at all.
