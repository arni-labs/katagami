Consider the keeper who wrote his nights down twice. Forty years he tended the flame, and forty years he set beside the plain record a second, gilded one, as though the sea were not enough unless he made it grander than it was. Ask thyself why. A thing seen truly needed no ornament; yet the man could not bear to leave it bare, and so he dressed each fog and each wreck in borrowed light, that some reader, coming after, might find him greater than his office. This is vanity. Thou dost the same, when thou tellest thy own day over to thyself in fairer words than it deserved. Mark it and stop. What is a storm, that thou shouldst embellish it? What is thy labour, that it should be sung? Do the work; write it plain; let the next man judge. The town, finding the two hands in one book, quarrelled over which was the true keeper — the honest clerk or the poet. They are the same man, and both are dust. Praise not the one nor pity the other. Keep thou the plain entry in thy own breast, and burn the gilded page before it flatters thee. The Gods asked of him light, not eloquence. They ask no more of thee.
