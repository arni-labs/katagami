# Judge feedback — lighthouse keeper's double logbook

The draft has the bones right: it begins from a fault observed, measures it against duty, turns the correction inward, and keeps the archaic second person steady throughout. Where it drifts from the corpus:

- **Cut the em-dash.** The draft turns on one ("the true keeper — the honest clerk or the poet"). Nowhere in the corpus does Casaubon reach for a dash; he renders exactly this kind of appositive turn with a colon or semicolon ("as puppies, biting one another: or untoward children"). The dash is the single modern mark that gives the piece away — replace it with a colon.

- **The close consoles, and it shouldn't.** "They ask no more of thee" softens the demand and sends the reader off reassured. Aurelius ends on the cold fact and leaves you standing in it ("a loved error is the hardest of all to yield"; things are "neither thine, nor in thy power"). End on the sterner note — the reckoning, the dust, the office still owed — not on comfort.

- **Let the questions cascade instead of balancing.** The corpus piles rhetorical questions and chains them loosely with "and" until they spill over ("...so obscure, and so fallible? and our souls nothing but an exhalation of blood?"). The draft gives two clean, symmetrical questions and stops. Add a third and join them with "and" so they accumulate rather than sit as a tidy matched pair.

- **Loosen the neat antitheses.** "light, not eloquence," "Praise not the one nor pity the other," "the honest clerk or the poet" — the piece leans on crisp balanced opposition that reads more like modern aphorism than Casaubon, whose sentences wind and qualify through subordinate clauses before they land. Trade at least one of the clean balances for a longer, more sinuous clause.

- **Keep the maxims in the timeless present.** "A thing seen truly needed no ornament" and "fairer words than it deserved" push the general truth into the past tense. The corpus states its laws in the present ("A thing false yet steady will deceive thee"). Shift to "A thing seen truly needs no ornament."

- **Untangle the central image.** "light" does double duty — the false "borrowed light" of the ornament and the true "light" the Gods ask of the keeper. Using one word for both the vice and the duty blurs the point. Give the ornament its own word (gilding, borrowed colour) and reserve "light" for the keeper's real office.
