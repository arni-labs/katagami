October 3rd.--The bridge below the mill, of three stone arches, was thrown down by the flood of the twenty-ninth of last month, when the river rose, by the mark cut upon the mill-post, seven feet and some inches above its summer level in the space of a single night. I walked out this morning to see what the water had done, and found two arches wholly gone and the third standing but split through the keystone. The village is now divided upon the manner of the rebuilding. The elder men would have every stone replaced as it lay, the parapet at its former low height, the roadway of its former narrow width; the younger, who drive the heavier waggons, would have it raised and widened, and the piers set with their edges to the current, as I have seen done upon the larger rivers to the south. I observed that the arch which survived was the one whose pier stood square against the stream, and not askew; from which it would appear that the old builders, whatever their intention, had left the parish a lesson written in the one stone the flood could not carry. Which counsel will prevail I am not yet able to say. It is a common thing that a people will restore what they have lost in the very shape of its losing, and reckon the faithful copy a virtue.
