The guidebook is eloquent upon the new bridge, and gives it three full pages where the cathedral got but one. It calls the arch a triumph of the modern engineer, declares that the old wooden crossing carried away by the flood was a rude and dangerous makeshift, and assures the traveler that the village rose as one man to demand a worthier structure in its place. We came a long way to admire this unanimity.

We found the village divided into two camps that do not speak. The one party holds that the bridge must be raised again exactly as it stood, plank for plank, because their grandfathers crossed it and now and then drowned off it, and that is history. The other party holds that a bridge which drowns people ought to be improved upon, which struck the stranger as a bold and even reckless opinion. Between the two the river runs unbridged, and has run so for fourteen months, and the people ford it at the shallows and quarrel across the water.

I took the liberty of admiring the plans for the new stone arch, and was informed by a man on the near bank that I was a meddling fool, and by a man on the far bank that I was a wise and traveled gentleman, and I could not for the life of me tell which of the two I had agreed with.
