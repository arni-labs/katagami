Dear Sir,—You desire my account of the bridge below our village, and of the long contention it has raised among us; and though the matter belongs rather to the humours of a parish than to natural history, I will gladly set down what I have myself observed.

The old bridge, a low thing of oak much patched in my time, was carried off entire in the great flood of two winters since, when the brook, which in dry summers a child may step across, rose in a single night above the meadow hedges. Since that ruin the parish has fallen into two parties. Our elder men will have it rebuilt precisely as it stood, in the very timber and upon the very piers, holding that what served the fathers must serve the sons; while the younger sort, and some reason is on their side, would raise it in stone and set it a full yard higher, that the next flood may pass beneath it and not above.

Having measured the old piers where they yet stand, I find them planted below the common water-mark, which no prudent builder would now allow; so that the reverence paid to the ancient work seems to me misplaced, the ancients having built ill. Whether affection or prudence shall carry the day I dare not foretell, having watched custom outlive far better arguments than these. I remain, &c.
