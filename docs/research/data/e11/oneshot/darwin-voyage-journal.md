November 22nd.--We halted for the night at a small village seated upon both banks of a rapid stream, and crossed by a bridge of stone which the last great flood had in part destroyed. The waters, I was told, had risen in a few hours to a height of nine feet, sweeping away the two central arches, and leaving the roadway suspended above the gap. The inhabitants were then engaged in the work of rebuilding, and much divided in their counsel. One party desired that the bridge should be restored precisely as it had stood, from an attachment to the form their fathers had raised; the other, that it should be made higher, and of a single wide arch, so that the floods of future years might pass without obstruction. It was curious to observe how the argument turned less upon the river than upon the past. I have remarked elsewhere, among people of this kind, a singular unwillingness to amend what long custom has established, even where the advantage of the change is plain. The masons, meanwhile, laboured upon the old foundations, as being the readier course. Whether the bridge would rise in its former shape, or in the better one, I had not the means of learning; for we were compelled to move forward at daybreak, and I left the question, like many another, unresolved behind me.
