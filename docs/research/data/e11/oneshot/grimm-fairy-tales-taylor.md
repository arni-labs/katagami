There was once a village that stood upon both banks of a river, and a wooden bridge joined the two halves, and had joined them longer than any grandfather could say. One spring the rains came without stopping for seven days and seven nights, and on the seventh night the river rose and took the bridge away, so that the village was cut in two, and the people on the near bank could not come to the people on the far bank.

Now in that village there lived two brothers, a carpenter and a mason, and each of them wished to build the bridge again. The carpenter said, 'We will raise it of wood, plank for plank, just as our fathers raised it, for that is the way a bridge should be.' But the mason said, 'We will raise it of stone, high above the flood, so that no water shall ever take it away again.' And they quarrelled, and neither would give way to the other.

So the elders called them both and said, 'Let each build his own half, and we shall see.' The carpenter built his half of wood, and it was pretty to look upon, and the very next flood carried it off. The mason built his half of stone, and it stood. Then the whole village built in stone as the mason had done, and the bridge has stood to this day, and if it has not fallen, it is standing there yet.
