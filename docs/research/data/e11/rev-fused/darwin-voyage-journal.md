November 22nd.--We halted for the night at a small village seated upon both banks of a rapid stream, where a bridge of stone had lately been broken by a flood. In the morning I walked down to examine it. Two of the central arches were wholly carried away; the third stood, though its parapet was gone, and upon the mill-post I found the high-water mark cut fresh, five feet above the summer level. The surviving pier, I observed, was the single one set square against the current, the others having stood obliquely to it. The inhabitants were much divided in their counsel. One party would have the bridge restored precisely as it stood, from an attachment to the form their fathers raised; the other would have it made higher, and of a single wide arch, that the floods of future years might pass without obstruction. It was curious to remark how the argument turned less upon the river than upon the past. The masons, meanwhile, laboured on the old foundations, as being the readier course; and the plain lesson of the pier, that the square face had outlasted the oblique, seemed to weigh with none of them. Whether the bridge would rise in its former shape, or in the better one, I could not learn. We were to march at daybreak, and I stayed too short a time to see the issue.
