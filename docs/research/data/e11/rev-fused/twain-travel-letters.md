The guidebook devotes eleven pages to the new bridge and a single grudging paragraph to the cathedral. It calls the arch "a triumph of the modern engineer, before which the traveler must stand humbled and bareheaded"; it names the old wooden crossing, carried off by the flood, "a rude and perilous makeshift unworthy of so enlightened a commune"; and it assures us the village rose as one man to demand a nobler structure. I stood before the spot bareheaded, as instructed. There was no bridge.

There had been none for nineteen months. The village was divided into two camps that did not speak. One party held the bridge must go up again exactly as it stood, plank for plank, because their grandfathers had crossed it and now and then drowned off it, and that was history. The other held that a bridge which drowns a mere thirty or forty souls in a generation might, without impiety, be improved upon. Between them the river ran on, unbridged and in no hurry, and the people forded it at the shallows and quarreled across the water.

I admired the plans aloud. A man on the near bank told me he had never heard a greater fool talk; a man on the far bank told me he had never met a wiser head; and I agreed warmly with both, which is the entire art of travel.
