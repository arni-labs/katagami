Dear Sir,—You desire my account of our bridge below the village, and of the long contention its ruin has bred among us; and though the business belongs rather to the humours of a parish than to natural history, I shall gladly set down what I have myself observed.

The old bridge, a low thing of oak much patched with tar in my time, was carried off entire in the flood of last Candlemas, when the brook that in dry summers a child may step across rose in one night above the meadow hedges. You will recall your own opinion, that running water seldom stirs a well-set stone; yet I have measured these piers where they yet stand, and find them to be bedded below the common water-mark, so that every winter's spate works at their footings. Our elder men would have all raised again as it was; the younger would set it a full yard higher, and in stone. Which of the two, affection or prudence, shall carry I dare not say.

I may add a matter more to your purpose than to theirs. A white-breasted water-ousel that haunted the old arches has not forsaken them: all through this hard March she dips and sings among the fallen stones, and the wagtails are already at their building in the crevices. What most strikes me is not that the arch fell, but that the birds keep their station over the wreck of it, and grieve the ruin less than we. I remain, &c.
