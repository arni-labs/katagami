October 3rd.--The bridge below the mill, of three stone arches, was thrown down by the flood of the twenty-ninth of last month, when the river rose seven feet and some inches above its summer level in a single night, by the mark cut upon the mill-post. I walked out this morning to see what the water had done. Two arches are wholly gone; the third stands, but is split through the keystone. Along the near bank the flood has laid a wrack of hazel and torn turf, and in it I counted the shells of some four species of snail brought down from the chalk above. The village is divided upon the manner of the rebuilding. The elder men will have every stone replaced as it lay, the parapet at its former low height; the younger, who drive the heavier waggons, would raise it and widen it, and set the piers edgewise to the current. The one arch that stood was the arch whose pier faced square upstream: the two set askew are gone. Which counsel will prevail I am not yet able to say. It is common that a people will restore what they have lost in the very shape of its losing, as a swallow, turned out from the eave, will build the next year on the selfsame beam her old nest was taken from.
