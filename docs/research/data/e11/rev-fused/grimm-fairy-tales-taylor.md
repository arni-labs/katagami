There was once a village that stood upon both banks of a river, joined by a bridge, until a flood rose for three days and three nights and carried it away, so that the village was cut in two. On the far bank there sat an old woman, wet to the bone and begging, who was the river's own self in a beggar's shape, though not a soul knew it.

First came a rich man to build the bridge anew, and the old woman called to him: "Lay but one stone for me, and your bridge shall stand; turn me away, and the water shall have you." He only laughed. "I build for the market, not for beggars"; and he raised a proud arch of dressed stone, which the river took in the night, and him along with it.

Then came his brother, no kinder than the first, who would keep the whole toll; and when the old woman begged of him, she was turned away as before, so that his bridge stood but a day and the next flood took it.

Last of all came a poor mason who had nothing but his two hands, and when the old woman begged, he said: "Gladly, good mother, and here is my coat against the cold." So he laid her stone first of all and built the rest upon it; and the water sank down and grew still, and let him work in peace. That bridge has never fallen; and if it has not fallen, it is standing there yet.
