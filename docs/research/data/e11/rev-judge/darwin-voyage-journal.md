November 22nd.--We halted for the night at a small village seated upon both banks of a rapid stream, crossed by a bridge of stone which the last flood had in great part destroyed. I measured the marks the water had left upon the mill-wall: it had risen eleven feet four inches above the summer level, and in a single night. Two of the four arches were gone. The third stood whole; the fourth was breached at the crown. I remarked that the one entire arch was that whose pier stood square to the current, while the two carried away had been set askew, their cut-waters half turned down the stream. The stone was a coarse red sandstone, much worn, bedded in a lime so decayed that I crushed a fragment between finger and thumb.

The inhabitants were then rebuilding, and divided in their counsel. One party would have every stone replaced as it had lain; the other would raise the roadway and throw a single wider arch, that the floods to come might pass beneath it. The dispute, I observed, turned less upon the river than upon the past. Which plan would prevail I could not learn. We were ordered forward at daybreak, and I had no second morning to see the work advanced.
