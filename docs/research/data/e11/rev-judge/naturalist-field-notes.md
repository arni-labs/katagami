October 3rd.--The bridge below the mill, of three stone arches, was thrown down by the flood of the twenty-ninth of last month, when the river rose, by the mark cut upon the mill-post, seven feet and some inches above its summer level in a single night. I walked out this morning to see what the water had done. Two arches were wholly gone; the third stood, but was split through the keystone. The pier of the standing arch had been set square against the current; the two that fell stood askew to it. The willows on the near bank were combed flat downstream, and the wrack of hay lodged in their forks marked the flood a full yard above the meadow. A water-ousel worked the shallows, dipping among the fallen stones as though the ruin were no concern of its own.

The village is divided: the elder men would have every stone replaced as it lay, at its former height and width; the younger, who drive the heavier waggons, would raise and widen it, and set the piers edge-on to the stream. I have watched the swallows return each spring to a barn whose eaves were stopped against them, beating the old wall a season before they seek another; men keep to a lost form in the same way, from a habit that outlives its cause. Which counsel will prevail I am not yet able to say. It is a common thing that a people will restore what they have lost in the very shape of its losing.
