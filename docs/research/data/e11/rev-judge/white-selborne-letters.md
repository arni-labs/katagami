Dear Sir,—Your last letter, with its account of the herons come back to your water-meadows, gave me much pleasure; and you ask, besides, for the history of our broken bridge, which I will set down as it stands, though it belongs more to the humours of a parish than to natural history.

The old bridge of oak was carried off entire in the flood of last Candlemas, when our brook, which in a dry July a child may step across, rose in one night above the alder-roots and into the lower orchard. I have the swifts of that week in my journal: they came on the third of May, five days later than the year before, the spring being backward and cold. You will remember that Ray reckons the swift among the birds that lie torpid in winter; but I have watched ours quit us clean in the first week of August, and I hold with you that they are gone over sea. As to the bridge, our elder men would raise it again in the old timber, upon the old low piers; the younger sort would have it in stone. Measuring the piers where they yet stand, I find them set a little below the common water-mark; but more than that I will not affirm, for one dry season is a poor witness. Whether affection or prudence shall carry the day I dare not foretell, having watched too many settled habits outlast their cause. I remain, &c.
