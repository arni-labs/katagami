The guidebook is warm upon the subject of the new bridge. "Where the flood of '67 swept away a mean and rotten crossing," it declares, "the awakened spirit of the village has reared a stone triumph worthy of the Caesars, and every heart from bank to bank beat as one to raise it." Every heart beat as one. We came a good many miles to admire this concord.

The village has beaten as one for three years, four months, and eleven days, which is the precise age of the quarrel, and in all that time it has not laid a single stone. One party will have the bridge back exactly as it drowned their grandfathers, plank for plank, out of respect. The other party, a reckless and freethinking faction, holds that a bridge ought not to drown people at all. Between these two philosophies the river runs bare, and the citizens ford it to their own funerals. They do not build the bridge; they debate it.

I made the tactical blunder of admiring the plans. A gentleman on the near bank at once pronounced me a meddling foreign jackass, and a gentleman on the far bank, with equal heat, a sage and a traveled mind. I bowed to both, agreed with both, and understood neither, which is the whole and perfect science of travel: to admire a thing enormously and take particular care never to find out what it is.
