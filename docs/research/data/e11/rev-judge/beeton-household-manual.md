TO REBUILD THE VILLAGE BRIDGE AFTER A FLOOD.

MATERIALS.--40 tons of dressed stone, 12 oak baulks each 20 feet long, 8 bushels of lime, 3 cart-loads of sharp gravel, 1 master mason, 6 steady labourers, and the old plan kept in the vestry chest.

Mode.--The flood having carried away three arches in one August night, wait for the river to fall and the bed to dry before a stone is laid. Clear the ruin first. Save every squared block the water has spared, for these are cheaper than the quarry. Drive the piles for the centring into firm gravel, not into silt, and wedge them till they will not stir. Set the timber centres true, and prove each with the level. Raise the springing a full foot above the old flood-mark, that the next spate pass beneath the bridge. Bed every stone in lime worked to the thickness of a crown-piece, and drive no course home till the one below has taken. Key the arch from both haunches at once. Strike the centres only when the mortar is hard. Let no course be hurried. The old folk would have it built as it stood; build it higher.

Time.--Two months for the work; a whole winter of talk before a spade is lifted.

Average cost, sixty pounds, the saved stone and borrowed centring reckoned in.

Seasonable in late summer, when the water runs low and the days are long for masons.

Note.--Build once and build high; a bridge set back to its old height is a bridge already half lost.
