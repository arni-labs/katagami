There was once a village that stood upon both banks of a river, and a little bridge of stone joined the one bank to the other. In that village lived a rich miller who was greedy and hard, and a poor widow, and the widow's young son, who was kind. One year the rains fell for seven days and seven nights, and on the seventh night the river swelled and carried the bridge away; and the water spoke as it went, saying, 'Whoever builds me a fair arch again shall have my blessing, and whoever grudges it shall have my curse.'

The miller was asked first, for he was the richest. 'Let the poor wade,' he said, and shut his door, and went back to the counting of his gold. Then the elders were asked, and they quarrelled the whole winter through and did nothing. At last the widow's son came, though he had but two hands and not a coin to his name. He gathered the fallen stones from the shallows, and an old grey fish rose in the water and showed him where each one lay, and by spring the bridge stood fairer than before.

Then the river kept its word. It carried gold down from the hills and laid it at the widow's door, so that she and her son wanted for nothing ever after. But the miller rose one morning to find his wheel still and his hoard turned to river-mud. And if the bridge has not fallen, it is standing there yet.
