October 3rd.--Our bridge below the mill, of three stone arches, was thrown down by a flood on the twenty-ninth of last month, when the river rose, by a mark cut on the mill-post, seven feet and some inches above its summer level in the space of a single night. I walked out this morning to see what the water had done, and found that two arches were wholly gone, and a third standing, but split through the keystone. The village is now divided upon the rebuilding: our elder men are for replacing every stone as it lay, the parapet low as before, the roadway narrow as of old; the younger sort, who drive heavier waggons, are for raising and widening it, and setting the piers edge-on to the current, as may be seen on larger rivers to the south. I observed that the arch which stood was that whose pier lay square against the stream, and not askew; from which it appears that the old builders, whatever their intention, had left the parish much of a lesson, written in a single stone. Which counsel will prevail I am not yet able to say. It is a common thing that a people will restore what they have lost in the very shape of its losing, and reckon the faithful copy a virtue.
