Dear Sir,—You desire my account of the bridge below our village, and of the long contention it raised among us; and though the matter belongs rather to the humours of a parish than to the natural history of birds, I will gladly set down what I have myself observed.

The old bridge, a low thing of oak much patched with tar in my time, was carried off entire in the great flood of two winters since, when the brook, which in dry summers a child may step over, rose in a single night above the meadow hedges. Since that ruin the parish is fallen into two parties: our elder men are for rebuilding it precisely as it stood, in the very timber and upon the very piers, holding that what served the fathers should in every age serve the sons; while the younger sort, and they have some reason on their side, would raise the new work in stone, and set it a full yard higher, that the next flood may pass beneath and not above.

Having measured the old piers where they yet stand, I find them planted below the common water-mark, which no prudent builder could now allow; so that the reverence paid to the ancient work seems to me to be misplaced: the ancients, whatever their care, built ill. Whether affection or prudence shall carry the day I dare not foretell; but I have watched custom outlive far better arguments than these, and will hazard no more. I remain, &c.
