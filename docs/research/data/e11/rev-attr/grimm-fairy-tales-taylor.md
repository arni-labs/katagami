There was once a village that stood upon both banks of a river; and a wooden bridge had joined the two halves longer than any grandfather could say. One spring the rains fell without stopping for seven days and seven nights, and on the seventh night the river rose and swept the bridge away, so that the village was cut in two: those on the near bank could not come to those on the far bank.

Now in that village lived two brothers, a carpenter and a mason, and each wished to raise the bridge again. The carpenter said: 'We will build it of wood, plank for plank, just as our fathers did, for that is the way a bridge should be.' But the mason answered him: 'We will build it of stone, high above the flood, so that no water shall carry it off again.' And they quarrelled, and neither would give way.

Then they went to an old grandmother, older than the bridge, and she said: 'You are both fools; let each raise his own half, and the river shall be your judge.' So the carpenter made his half of wood, and it was pretty to look upon; and when the next flood came, it carried his work down into the water. But the mason set his half in stone, and it stood where he had set it. Then the whole village brought their stone, and built as she had said; and the bridge stands to this day, and if it has not fallen, it is standing yet.
