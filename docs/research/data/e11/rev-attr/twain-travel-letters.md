The guidebook is eloquent upon the new bridge, and gives it three full pages where the cathedral got but one. It calls the arch a triumph of the modern engineer; it swears the old wooden crossing, carried off by the flood, was a rude and dangerous makeshift; it assures the traveler that the village rose as one man to demand a worthier thing in its place. We came a hundred miles to admire this unanimity. There is none.

We found instead two camps of villagers who will not speak to each other. One party holds the bridge must go up again exactly as it stood, plank for plank, because their grandfathers crossed it, drowned off it now and then, and that, they say, is history. The other party holds a bridge which drowns five people in a winter might have been improved upon. He is thought a reckless fellow, the man who says so. Between the two the river runs unbridged, and has run so for fourteen months; the people ford it at the shallows and quarrel across the water.

I made bold to admire the plans for the new stone arch. A man on the near bank heard me out, and then informed me I was a meddling fool; a man on the far bank swore I was a wise and traveled gentleman. I could not for the life of me tell which of them I had agreed with.
