November 22nd.--We halted for the night at a small village seated on both banks of a rapid stream, and crossed by a bridge of stone which the last great flood had carried away in part. The waters, I was told, rose in a few hours to a height of nine feet, sweeping off the two central arches, and leaving the roadway suspended above the gap, with the parapet broken on either hand. The inhabitants were then engaged in the work of rebuilding, and much divided in their counsel. Some desired that the bridge should be restored precisely as it stood, from an attachment to the form their fathers raised; the others, that it should be made higher, and of a single wide arch, so that the floods of future years, when they came, might pass without obstruction. It was curious to observe how the argument turned less on the river than on the past. There is, among people of this kind, a very singular unwillingness to amend what long custom has established, even where the advantage of the change is plain. The masons, meanwhile, laboured on the old foundations, as being the readier course. Whether it would rise in its former shape, or in the better one, I had not the means of learning; for we were compelled to move forward at daybreak, and I left the question, like many another, unresolved behind me.
