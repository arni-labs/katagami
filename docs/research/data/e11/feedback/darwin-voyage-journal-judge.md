# Revision feedback — Darwin voyage journal

Judged by ear against the corpus (the beetles-at-sea passage) and the known-good San Fernando clock replica.

- Everything in the draft is secondhand. "The waters, I was told, had risen..."; "The inhabitants were then engaged..." The narrator relays but never measures. Darwin's authority comes from what he took in with his own instruments — the net drawn up, the specimens counted and sorted into named genera, the chronometer compared against the tower ("I was surprised to find, on comparing the hour... that the clock stood full four minutes in advance"). Put the observer's own eye and rule on at least one fact here, rather than crediting all of it to hearsay. The contract's own Never warns against omitting the conditions of observation.

- The particulars are too few and too soft for Darwin. His register is thick with named, catalogued specifics — the genera listed out, 370 miles, eight species of which four aquatic. The draft offers "nine feet" and "two central arches" and then drifts into "people of this kind" and "long custom." "I have remarked elsewhere" is precisely the vagueness he avoids; he names the elsewhere. Ground the reflection in measured, placed detail instead of general social observation.

- The balance tips too early and too far into interpretation. Darwin describes at length before he ventures a cautious inference; the draft gives a brief description and then spends most of its length on the character of the villagers. Describe the bridge, the flood, the surviving pier concretely first, and let the generalization arrive late and lightly, the way "it appeared to me most probable" closes his beetle passage.

- "I left the question, like many another, unresolved behind me" is an elegiac, writerly sign-off — the model reaching for pathos. Darwin's admissions are flatter and more mechanical: "I could not learn. I had stayed too short a time to observe the issue." Strip the romance from the ending; report the plain reason he could not see how it turned out.

- The whole entry is a single paragraph of uniformly long, serpentine sentences with almost no short beat to break them. Darwin swings — the long analytic sentence answered by a short flat one ("One day... I paid particular attention to this subject"; the replica's "The effect was more singular than I had looked for."). Introduce a couple of short declaratives to give the prose his cadence.

- What lands: the dated-and-placed opening ("November 22nd.--We halted for the night at a small village seated upon both banks of a rapid stream"), the framing of the dispute as turning "less upon the river than upon the past," and the sober refusal to predict the outcome are all in his key. Keep them; the work is to trade the essayist's generality and the romantic close for the measurer's concrete particulars.
