# Revision feedback — Grimm, fairy tales (Taylor, 1823)

The formulaic opening, the "seven days and seven nights," the direct speech, and the closing tag ("and if it has not fallen, it is standing there yet") are all genuinely Grimm in sound. But the tale underneath is the wrong shape:

- **It is built on a two, where the form demands a three.** Two brothers, two materials, two halves — a binary debate, not the triadic escalation that is the voice's central move ("repeat the pattern the third time with the change"). The excerpt marches in threes (oven, apple tree, then the house with the old woman); the replica climbs the tower three times ("the first time they laughed... the second time they frowned... but the third time..."). A wood-versus-stone opposition reads as parable, not märchen. Rebuild it to turn three times.

- **The tale argues a case instead of simply happening.** The whole engine is a proposition being proved — stone outlasts the flood, therefore build in stone. The characters supply reasons for their positions ("for that is the way a bridge should be"; "so that no water shall ever take it away again"). Grimm's justice is "exact, unexamined": events fall in order and the outcome is not defended. Here the outcome is an argument won, which brushes against "never interprets the tale for the listener."

- **There is no marvel in it.** The corpus world is enchanted — loaves that cry "Take us out, take us out," an apple tree that begs to be shaken. The draft is a wholly naturalistic engineering dispute wearing fairy-tale cadence; nothing speaks, nothing transforms, the flood is mere weather rather than a marvelous agent. The wonder the voice is made of never appears.

- **No wicked party means no justice to deal out.** "Never spares the wicked their due" has nothing to act on: the carpenter is not wrong, only unlucky, and his half simply washes away. The tale settles a fortune by practicality, not by desert — so the moral machinery the form runs on sits idle.

- **The closing formula is right in sound but hung on the wrong kind of ending.** "If it has not fallen, it is standing there yet" is a true Grimm tag, but it caps a pragmatic result (the better material won) rather than a fate earned by virtue or fault. The settled-fortune close should land on someone's deserved end, not on a construction verdict.
