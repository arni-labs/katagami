# Revision feedback — Twain, travel letters (1869)

The draft has the right skeleton (guidebook's boast, the traveler's evidence, the gap) and a genuinely Twain-shaped self-implicating close. Where it misses the voice:

- **The comic figure — Twain's chief weapon in this corpus — is missing entirely.** The excerpt does its damage by piling up absurdly exact numbers delivered with a straight face ("six millions of francs in ounces and carats alone," "fifty millions of francs"), and the known-good replica rides "four minutes fast for upwards of thirty years" as a running gag. The draft's numbers ("three full pages," "fourteen months") are merely informational; none is deployed as the punchline. Find the figure the situation makes ridiculous and hammer it.

- **The joke is signaled a beat early.** "We came a long way to admire this unanimity" winks before the reader has seen anything to be ironic about — it delivers the verdict ahead of the evidence, inverting the natural order (report the marvel straight, then supply the facts, then let the gap detonate). Twain reports the "village rose as one man" as flat fact and lets the next paragraph blow it up; the aside should not tip his hand first.

- **The guidebook is summarized, not quoted against itself.** The excerpt reproduces inflated claims in their own puffed-up voice so they hang themselves. The draft paraphrases ("a triumph of the modern engineer," "a rude and dangerous makeshift") but never lets the guidebook's actual purple sentence stand on the page long enough to be demolished. Give it real rope.

- **The sentences run too even.** Every paragraph is built of long, comma-strung coordinate clauses that all move at one pace; there is no short deadpan snap dropped in for the kill (the replica's "They do not correct the clock; they correct themselves."). The final sentence is another long run where a curt one would land harder.

- **The diction never collides registers.** Twain needles by setting a plain Anglo-Saxon word beside a mock-grand one ("gew-gaws," "tin trumpery," "consummate ass," "peradventure"). The draft's vocabulary is competent and period-smooth but reaches for no absurd inflated noun as a barb; the mockery stays in the structure and never gets into the words.

- **The self-confession lands soft.** The narrator ends up looking foolish by accident ("could not for the life of me tell which of the two I had agreed with"), but Twain generalizes his own folly into a law about the whole tribe of tourists ("which is the entire art of the tourist"; "what a consummate ass he can become"). Push the ending past personal befuddlement into the indictment of the traveler's posture that the voice is built to deliver.
