Consider the keeper who wrote his nights down twice. Forty years he tended the lamp, and forty years he set beside the plain record a second book, gilded and grand, as though the sea he watched were too poor a thing to enter as it was. Ask thyself why he did it. A thing seen truly needs no gilding; yet the man could not bear to leave his page bare, and so he dressed each fog, and each wreck, and each long uneventful dark, in colours it never wore, that some reader coming after might find him larger than his office. This is vanity, and thou art not free of it. For dost thou not tell thy own day over to thyself in fairer words than it earned? and number thy small labours as though the world attended them? and call a dull watch a hardship, that thou mightest seem to have borne much? What is a storm, that thou shouldst adorn it? What is the trimming of a wick, that it should be sung? Do the work; write it as it was; let him who comes after weigh it. The town, finding two hands in one book, fell to quarrelling over which was the true keeper: the honest clerk, or the poet. Both are already dust, and the sea forgets them equally. Keep the plain entry, and burn the gilded one. The Gods gave thee an office, and will ask of thee its light.
