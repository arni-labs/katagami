Write each fact once. The keeper of the Varde light kept his logbook for forty years, and every night he set down the weather twice: once as it was, once as he wished it read. 'Fog after midnight, three vessels warned' stood the plain entry. Beneath it ran the other: the fog came down like a shroud upon the drowned, and the lamp threw its lonely defiance against the deep. One sentence tells the reader what happened. Its twin tells him what the keeper felt, and buries the fact under the feeling. A record serves the man who reads it after. He wants the hour, the wind, the count of ships; he does not want the keeper's mood. When the town found the double book, it argued for a season over which column to trust. The plain one they could act upon. Its ornament they could only admire. Admiration is not information. If you must praise the sea, praise it in a letter and keep it out of the log, for the log has one duty and it is to be believed. Our keeper wrote well in both hands. He erred only in writing the same night twice. Choose the sentence that carries the fact, and strike the other.
